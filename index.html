Abstract

This book traces a single continuous path from the breaking of symmetry 13.8 billion years ago to the question of how you know when you are wrong. It claims that reality as you experience it is constructed — not arbitrarily, not by conspiracy, not by any single authority, but through a traceable chain of emergence that can be examined, tested, and understood. The same structural principle operates at every level of this chain: variation under constraint, maintained by feedback, viable only at the boundary between rigidity and dissolution.

The master principle: every system that stays open to correction survives. Every system that closes, dies. This is not a moral preference. It is thermodynamics applied to information.

Part One: The Physical Foundation

Chapter 1: Something Rather Than Nothing

From symmetry breaking through stellar nucleosynthesis to the chemical alphabet of life. Perfect symmetry contains no information. For anything to exist, symmetry must break. The periodic table maps constraints governing atomic combination — lawful combination under constraint produces structures that persist. Carbon's four bonding sites create the backbone of molecular complexity through quantum mechanical constraints.

Chapter 2: The Antagonist

Entropy, thermodynamics, and the second law as structural antagonist. Life is the local reversal of entropy — open systems maintaining internal order by importing energy and exporting waste. Viable systems exist at the edge of chaos, between the crystal (rigid order) and the gas (formless chaos). Entropy operates at every level: identities, institutions, civilizations.

Chapter 3: From Chemistry to Creatures

Abiogenesis, evolution as feedback loop (variation under constraint filtered by selection), and the emergence of prediction through nervous systems. Memory as externalized experience. Emotion as adaptive state regulation. The bridge from chemistry to biology to prediction to mind.

Part Two: The Prediction Engine

Chapter 4: The Prediction Engine

Karl Friston's free energy principle: self-organizing systems minimize surprise or dissipate. Andy Clark's predictive processing: information flows inside-out, with only prediction errors propagating upward. Anil Seth's controlled hallucination: perception is the brain's best guess constrained by sensory feedback. Convergence with Kahneman's dual-process theory and Boyd's OODA loop. The metabolic cost of prediction explains cognitive compression — habits, heuristics, biases as energy-saving measures.

Chapter 5: The Body

Embodiment provides stakes, urgency, consequence. Qualia as functional interface between body and model. The hard problem of consciousness dissolved through continuity analogy with chemistry-to-biology transition. Pragmatic stance on unverifiable questions. Ryle's category mistake applied to consciousness.

Part Three: From Individual Mind to Shared World

Chapter 6: Cooperation

Tomasello's shared intentionality: recursive modeling of other minds as cognitive foundation of social life. Axelrod's iterated prisoner's dilemma: trust as mathematically optimal strategy. Tajfel's minimal group paradigm: in-group preference as structural tension within cooperation.

Chapter 7: Memory and the Self

Memory as reconstruction, not retrieval (Loftus). The self as center of narrative gravity (Dennett). The Helen Keller case: awareness exists without language, selfhood requires language, identity is constructed, construction event precisely datable. Descartes correction: "I word, therefore I know what I am."

Chapter 8: Language

Language as medium of selfhood, architecture of abstract thought, technology enabling collective prediction. Vygotsky's inner speech. Sapir-Whorf linguistic relativity. Lakoff and Johnson's conceptual metaphor theory. Language as simultaneously liberation (hooks) and domination (Orwell, Foucault).

Part Four: How Selves and Societies Are Constructed

Chapter 9: Habit and Free Will

Kahneman's System 1 as compiled prediction. Free will as linguistically mediated interruption of automatic processing — compatibilist framework. Ego depletion and finite deliberative resources. Naming as prerequisite for pattern interruption.

Chapter 10: Institutions

Institutions as externalized prediction engines — compiled cognition. North's rules of the game, Weber's iron cage. Institutional ossification through feedback loop closure. Ashby's law of requisite variety: controllers require response repertoire matching environmental disturbance variety.

Chapter 11: Propaganda and the Information Environment

Ellul's integration propaganda: changing background assumptions invisibly. Chomsky and Herman's manufacturing consent. Backfire effect: identity-linked beliefs strengthen under contradiction. Social media as attention-optimization exploiting prediction-error. Ostrom's commons governance principles applied to information environment.

Chapter 12: Technology

Technology as feedback-loop accelerator. Temporal gap between exponential technological change and incremental institutional adaptation. AI as externalization of prediction itself. Functional zombies: automated cognition without reflective choice. Feedback architecture determines whether technology serves life or accelerates dissolution.

Part Five: Ethics, Mortality, and Justice

Chapter 13: Ethics

Collapse of counterfactual reasoning necessitates ethical reconstruction. Two foundational commitments: reduce suffering (pre-symbolic, requires no narrative) and preserve agency (prerequisite for ethical action). Causal-historical ethics: "what systems produced this?" replaces "could they have done otherwise?" Adaptive ethics as iterative moral refinement.

Chapter 14: Death

Death as un-narratable edge. Heidegger's being-toward-death: finitude gives choice urgency. Frankl's logotherapy: meaning requires finitude. Pain as pre-symbolic constraint — body's veto on narrative construction. Mortality makes openness urgent: because time ends, closure is fatal.

Chapter 15: Justice

Retributive justice's dependence on failing counterfactuals. Restorative justice: address victim suffering, develop offender agency, repair community trust (Zehr). Structural justice: addressing systems that produced conditions for harm, not merely individuals within them.

Part Six: The Synthesis

Chapter 16: The Edge of Chaos

Kauffman's Boolean networks, Bak's self-organized criticality, Langton's cellular automata — convergent discovery that complex adaptive systems are viable only between order and chaos. Pattern traced at every level of the book. Honest assessment: probably both real and partially cognitive artifact, but convergence evidence is strong.

Chapter 17: The Master Principle

Stay open to correction. Every system that stays open survives; every system that closes dies. Thermodynamics applied to information. Equivalent formulations: Popper's falsifiability, Taleb's antifragility, Ashby's requisite variety. Seven practices for maintaining openness: externalize thinking, test predictions, seek genuine disagreement, distinguish updating from destabilizing, examine identity-linked beliefs, monitor coherence drift, build error-detection institutions.

Chapter 18: The Story That Knows It Is a Story

Recursive collapse: the prediction engine modeling its own modeling finds itself all the way down. Not nihilism but beginning of conscious authorship. Gödel's incompleteness applied to frameworks. Conscious construction: deliberate authorship of reality, held provisionally, tested continuously, revised as needed. "We are physical matter that determines for itself what matters."

Key Philosophical Contributions

Novel synthesis connecting: free energy principle (Friston), predictive processing (Clark), controlled hallucination (Seth), dual-process theory (Kahneman), OODA loop (Boyd), shared intentionality (Tomasello), narrative identity (Dennett), edge of chaos (Kauffman/Bak/Langton), institutional theory (North/Ashby), information environment theory (Ellul/Chomsky), restorative justice (Zehr), and existential philosophy (Heidegger/Frankl) into a single continuous emergence chain from physics to ethics.

Central claim: adaptive persistence is descriptive rather than normative, grounded in physics. The master principle — stay open to correction — is not a moral preference but a thermodynamic constraint on viable complex systems.
